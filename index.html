Mythology & Symbolism

This section delves into the foundational Hindu myths that shape the character of Chandra, revealing his divine origins and the allegorical tales that define his astrological symbolism of mind, emotions, and cyclical nature.

Genealogy & Origin

Chandra emerged from the cosmic mind of the Puruṣa and is famously known as the son of the great sage Atri and his devoted wife, Anasūyā. This parentage links him directly to the primordial forces of creation and intellect, establishing his role as the presiding deity of the mind (manas).

The 27 Nakṣatras

Chandra married the 27 daughters of the Prajāpati Dakṣa, who are the 27 Nakṣatras (lunar mansions). His favoritism towards Rohiṇī angered his other wives, leading Dakṣa to curse him with a wasting disease. This curse explains the waning of the Moon (Kṛṣṇa Pakṣa) and its subsequent waxing (Śukla Pakṣa) after the intervention of Śiva.

Samudra-manthana

During the Samudra-manthana, the great churning of the cosmic ocean by the Devas and Asuras, Chandra was one of the divine treasures (ratnas) that emerged. Śiva took him and placed him upon his head as an ornament, granting him divine status and protection. This association highlights the Moon's pure, sattvic nature.

Core Symbolism

In Vedic astrology, Chandra is the ultimate symbol of the mind, emotions, mother, nourishment, and fertility. His cyclical phases represent the rhythms of life, death, and rebirth. As the fastest-moving graha, he governs our daily moods, mental state (mānasthiti), and immediate responses to the world, reflecting the ever-changing nature of consciousness.

Historical Timeline of Lunar Study

Trace the intellectual lineage of lunar observation and interpretation in India, from the earliest Vedic hymns to the sophisticated mathematical treatises of the classical and medieval eras.

c. 1500 BCE

Ṛg-Veda

The earliest hymns invoke the Moon primarily as Soma, a divine, intoxicating drink and a deity. Its role is largely ritualistic and calendrical, marking time for sacrifices.

c. 1200 BCE

Vedāṅga Jyotiṣa

This ancillary Vedic text, attributed to Lagadha, is one of the earliest works on Indian astronomy. It establishes a formal system for calculating tithis (lunar days) and nakṣatras for ritual purposes.

c. 400 CE

Sūrya Siddhānta

A foundational text of classical Indian astronomy. Its chapters on the Moon provide sophisticated formulas for calculating lunar longitude, including corrections for anomaly and evection, marking a major leap in mathematical precision.

c. 550 CE

Bṛhat Saṃhitā

Authored by the great polymath Varāhamihira, this encyclopedic work contains detailed sections on lunar phenomena, including conjunctions, eclipses, and astrological interpretations of the Moon's position in various nakṣatras and its aspects.

c. 1150 CE

Siddhānta Śiromaṇi

Bhāskara II's masterpiece further refines lunar calculations. He introduces concepts like the equation of center with greater accuracy and provides detailed commentary on lunar motion, solidifying the mathematical framework for centuries to come.
